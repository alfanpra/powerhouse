Section 111 · Presence of Prescribed Illicit Drug

The principal drug-driving offence in NSW.

Under section 111 of the Road Transport Act 2013, it is an offence to drive a motor vehicle with a prescribed illicit drug (PID) present in your oral fluid, blood, or urine. The Crown does not need to prove you were impaired. The presence of the substance is the offence.

This is the central distinction between drug driving and the more familiar PCA regime for alcohol. PCA matters turn on a measured concentration. Drug driving under s.111 is zero tolerance — any detectable amount of a PID is enough to make out the charge.

The four prescribed illicit drugs

The standard Mobile Drug Test screens for four substances:

  • THC — the active component of cannabis.
  • MDMA — ecstasy.
  • Methamphetamine — ice, speed.
  • Cocaine.

The two other drug-driving offences

Section 111 is supported by two adjacent provisions in the Road Transport Act:

  • Section 112 — Driving Under the Influence (DUI). A separate offence where police can prove actual impairment by a drug, used particularly where no roadside sample was obtained. Maximum penalty matches high-range PCA.
  • Section 111A — Combined Drug + Alcohol. Where a driver returns both a positive breath analysis and a positive oral fluid test, the combined offence applies — and the Mandatory Alcohol Interlock Program kicks in regardless of the PCA range.

The MDT Process

From the swab to the charge.

Mobile Drug Testing in NSW follows a structured four-step process. The first three steps happen at the roadside; the fourth — the one that decides the charge — happens weeks later at the forensic laboratory.

Step 1 — The initial roadside swab

Police direct the driver to provide a sample of oral fluid using a single-use collection device. The sample is screened for the presence of THC, MDMA, methamphetamine, or cocaine. A positive result proceeds to step 2; a negative result ends the encounter.

Step 2 — Secondary test in the MDT vehicle

On a positive initial result, the driver is required to provide a second sample inside the MDT vehicle for confirmatory analysis. A positive second test triggers immediate consequences:

  • 24-hour roadside suspension of the driver's licence.
  • The sample is sealed and sent to the forensic laboratory.
  • The driver is released — but a court appearance is now expected.

Step 3 — Laboratory confirmation

The sample is analysed at the NSW Forensic and Analytical Science Service. Lab confirmation typically takes 4 to 6 weeks. The lab certificate becomes the central piece of Crown evidence at hearing.

Step 4 — Court Attendance Notice

If the lab confirms a positive result, police issue a Court Attendance Notice. The first appearance is usually 6–10 weeks after the original roadside test. This is the window in which the brief should be reviewed, the chain of custody examined, and the defence — or sentencing — strategy locked in.

Why this matters for defence

The strict-liability character of section 111 means challenges at hearing focus less on what you did and more on what the Crown can prove — the integrity of the sample, the chain of custody, the certification of the testing device, and procedural compliance at each step. A surprising number of drug-driving matters turn on points of process rather than points of fact.

Drug driving + drug possession — the common pairing

Where drugs are found in the vehicle on a roadside stop, a section 10 Drug Misuse and Trafficking Act possession charge will typically be laid alongside the section 111 RTA matter. The two matters are usually heard together — and a coordinated defence is essential. See our dedicated Drug Possession page for the full possession framework.

DUI vs section 111 — the distinction

Section 111 charges arise from a positive oral fluid test — evidence of presence regardless of impairment. Section 112 DUI requires the Crown to prove actual impairment by the drug — typically used after a crash, or where no usable oral fluid sample was obtained, or where the alleged impairment is severe enough to support a more serious charge.

Combined drink and drug driving

Where a driver returns both a positive PCA breath analysis and a positive oral fluid test, the matters can be combined under section 111A. The combined charge triggers an automatic Mandatory Alcohol Interlock Program order on conviction, regardless of the PCA range — a significant uplift on what either matter alone would attract.

Our Defence Approach

How we defend drug-driving matters.

The strict-liability character of section 111 narrows the defence space — but it does not close it. These are the angles we work first, and where most of our drug-driving results come from.

01
Section 10 strategyFor first-offence presence matters, a Section 10 dismissal preserves your licence, your record and your insurance position. The principal sentencing objective on most s.111 matters.
02
Chain of custody challengesFrom the roadside swab to the lab certificate — every step in the testing chain must be procedurally compliant. We test each link: device certification, sample sealing, transport, lab handling.
03
Honest and reasonable mistakeRare but real — where the substance entered the system through passive exposure, contamination, or unknowing consumption (a spiked drink). Requires evidence; high evidentiary bar.

Excluding evidence (s.138 Evidence Act)Where the stop, the test direction, or the procedural protections were not properly observed, we apply to have the test result excluded — meaning the charge collapses.

Disputing the certificateThe lab certificate is the central piece of Crown evidence. Where the certification, methodology, or analysis is open to challenge, we will brief experts and run the contested point.

Strategic plea with mitigationTreatment plans, character references, financial impact evidence, and credible context — every element that can move the Magistrate toward a Section 10 outcome.
