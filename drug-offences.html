Section 10 · DMT Act

Possession of a prohibited drug.

Under section 10(1) of the Drug Misuse and Trafficking Act 1985, a person is guilty of an offence if they have a prohibited drug in their possession. It sounds simple — but possession is a heavily worked legal concept and the Crown must prove every element.

What "possession" actually requires

The Crown must establish, beyond reasonable doubt, that you had:

  • Intentional physical custody or control of the substance.
  • Exclusion of others — practical control, not necessarily sole ownership.
  • Knowledge of the existence of the drug.
  • That the substance is, in fact, a prohibited drug.

In shared spaces — a hire car, a flatshare, a club table — the Crown still has to prove your knowledge and your control. Many possession matters fall apart on the first or third element alone.

Joint possession

Where the Crown alleges joint possession (e.g. drugs in a glovebox between driver and passenger), they must prove each accused person knew of the drugs and exercised control. The fact a drug was found near you is not, by itself, proof you possessed it.

Maximum penalty & sentencing

Possession is dealt with in the Local Court. The maximum penalty is 2 years imprisonment and/or a ,200 fine. In practice, the great majority of possession matters resolve well below the maximum — many with a Section 10 dismissal (no conviction) for first offenders with the right preparation.

Supply · Section 25 DMT Act

Supply is much broader than selling.

Under section 25 of the Drug Misuse and Trafficking Act 1985, "supply" is one of the widest concepts in NSW criminal law. The Crown does not need to prove a sale — and in many matters there is no sale at all.

"Supply" includes all of the following:

  • Selling and distributing
  • Agreeing to supply or offering to supply
  • Keeping or having drugs in possession for supply
  • Sending, forwarding, delivering or receiving for supply
  • Authorising, directing, causing, suffering, permitting any of the above
  • Attempting any of the above

Deemed supply

Section 29 of the Act introduces a critical evidentiary shortcut for the Crown — deemed supply. Where a person is found in possession of a quantity of drug equal to or above the traffickable threshold, they are deemed to possess for the purpose of supply unless they can prove (on the balance of probabilities) the drug was for personal use only.

Why quantity controls everything

The same offence — supply — carries radically different maximums depending on the quantity in your possession. The categories run small → traffickable → indictable → commercial → large commercial, and each tier escalates jurisdiction, penalty and sentencing principles.

NSW drug quantity thresholds (common drugs)

The thresholds that determine which tier you are charged under are set out in Schedule 1 of the Act. The common drugs are summarised below:

  • Cocaine, Heroin, Methamphetamine ("Ice") — small: 1g · traffickable: 3g · indictable: 5g · commercial: 250g · large commercial: 1kg.
  • MDMA / Ecstasy — small: 0.25g · traffickable: 0.75g · indictable: 1.25g · commercial: 125g · large commercial: 500g.
  • Cannabis leaf — small: 30g · traffickable: 300g · indictable: 1kg · commercial: 25kg · large commercial: 100kg.
Manufacture & Cultivation · Sections 23 & 24

Production, cultivation and the lab.

NSW treats the active production of prohibited drugs more seriously than possession or street-level supply. Section 23 covers cultivation of prohibited plants; section 24 covers the manufacture of drugs themselves.

What "manufacture" covers

"Manufacture" is given a broad statutory meaning. It includes the extraction, separation, purification or refining of a prohibited drug — so it covers everything from a clandestine lab synthesising methamphetamine to extracting cocaine from a precursor mixture. It is not limited to large-scale operations: even small-batch production can attract the indictable penalty range.

Cultivation — and the enhanced provisions

Cultivation of prohibited plants — primarily cannabis — is dealt with under section 23. The Act draws a critical distinction between standard cultivation and enhanced indoor cultivation (under section 23(2)) — meaning any cultivation using artificial light, nutrients, hydroponics, or the heat of an artificial source. Enhanced cultivation carries an extra five-year ceiling and is almost always indictable.

Aggravated manufacture

Where the manufacture takes place in the presence of a child (s.24A) or is for commercial purposes by a person in a position of authority, the Act applies aggravated penalty provisions on top of the base tier.

Importation · Commonwealth Jurisdiction

Crossing the border changes everything.

Drug importation is a federal offence — prosecuted under the Criminal Code Act 1995 (Cth), not NSW law. That means the Commonwealth Director of Public Prosecutions runs the matter, the maximum penalties are higher, and the evidentiary regime is different.

The principal provisions sit in Part 9.1 of the Criminal Code:

  • Section 307.1 — Importation of a commercial quantity of border-controlled drug. Maximum penalty: life imprisonment and/or $1.65 million fine.
  • Section 307.2 — Importation of a marketable quantity. Maximum penalty: 25 years imprisonment and/or $825,000 fine.
  • Section 307.3 — Importation of any quantity of border-controlled drug. Maximum penalty: 10 years imprisonment.

The "border-controlled drug" schedule

The Commonwealth maintains its own list of border-controlled drugs that mirrors but is not identical to the NSW schedule. Quantities are calibrated by pure rather than total weight in many cases — which itself can be a major issue at sentencing.

How importation matters are heard

Importation prosecutions are typically committed to the NSW District Court or, where the quantity warrants, the NSW Supreme Court. Both run as federal prosecutions before a State court. Sentencing is governed by federal law and applies the Crimes Act 1914 (Cth) rather than the NSW Crimes (Sentencing Procedure) Act 1999.

Why these are different matters

Importation matters often turn on knowledge — did the accused know they were importing, and did they know the substance was a border-controlled drug? Common scenarios include receipt of parcels via mail, attempted importation in luggage, controlled deliveries, and complicity in international syndicate operations. Each requires a different defence frame from the outset.

Our Defence Approach

How we defend drug matters.

Drug matters are won and lost at the edges of the Crown brief — search and seizure, knowledge, joint occupancy, certification of the substance. These are the angles we work first, and where most of our results come from.

01
Excluding evidence — s.138 Evidence ActWhere police searched without reasonable suspicion, conducted an improper field interview, or breached the procedural protections — we apply to have the drug evidence excluded entirely.
02
Knowledge — the Crown's burdenThe Crown must prove you knew of the drug's existence. Where the drug was found in shared space, an unattended bag, or a vehicle others used, knowledge is often the weakest element.
03
Joint occupancy / common areaDrugs found in a flatshare, hire car, or shared workspace — the Crown must prove your individual control, not just the geography. Strong defence where multiple people had access.
04
Disputing deemed supplySection 29 deems supply where quantity meets the traffickable threshold — but the accused can rebut on the balance of probabilities. Personal-use evidence, financial records, character all matter.
05
Type & quantity disputeThe Crown must positively prove the substance is a prohibited drug and that the weight is above the relevant threshold. We have run matters lost by the Crown on certification and chain-of-custody alone.
06
Sentencing strategy & Section 10Where the charge cannot be defended, we direct the matter at a Section 10 dismissal, Conditional Release Order, or other non-conviction outcome — backed by treatment, character, and a credible plan.
