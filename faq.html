02 · The Legal Process

From charge to court date.

What actually happens after you are charged — and how the case moves through the system.

You will be issued a Court Attendance Notice (CAN) with a date for your first appearance — usually in the Local Court. Between now and that date, your lawyer obtains the police brief, advises you on the strength of the prosecution case, and decides with you whether to plead guilty, not guilty, or negotiate the charges. Get legal advice before you enter any plea.
A mention is a brief listing where the court checks on the progress of your matter — for example, whether the brief has been served, or whether you are ready to enter a plea. For most Local Court mentions, your lawyer can appear for you and you do not need to attend. We will always tell you in advance when your attendance is required.
It depends on the charge and the court. A simple Local Court matter resolved on a plea may finish in 2–4 months. A defended Local Court hearing usually takes 6–9 months. District Court trials commonly run 12–18 months from committal. At our first meeting we give you a realistic timetable for your specific matter.
Never enter a plea without legal advice. A guilty plea is an admission to every element of the offence and to the police facts as they stand. We frequently negotiate amended facts or downgraded charges with the prosecutor — changes that can mean the difference between a conviction and a non-conviction outcome, or between a fine and a custodial sentence.
On a guilty plea, no — sentence submissions are made by your lawyer. On a defended hearing or trial, you have the right to give evidence but you are never obliged to. That is one of the key strategic decisions we make with you after reviewing the brief, and we prepare you carefully if the call is to go into the box.
The fact sheet is the prosecution's version, and the court sentences on the facts you accept. If the fact sheet overstates what happened, we negotiate amendments with the prosecutor before the plea — and if no agreement is reached, we can ask the court to hold a disputed facts hearing. Getting the facts right is often the single most important step on a plea.
03 · Bail, Police & Arrest

Your rights when police get involved.

What to do if police want to interview you, how bail works, and the consequences of breaching it.

With very limited exceptions, no. You generally have the right to silence — you are only required to provide your name and address. A "voluntary" interview is rarely in your interest. Politely decline, then call us before you say anything else. Anything you say in interview can be played to a jury.
Bail is your release from custody while your case is being decided, on the condition that you appear at court and comply with any conditions imposed. Bail can be granted by police at the station or by the court. The decision is governed by the Bail Act 2013 (NSW) and turns on whether you pose any unacceptable risk if released.
Yes, but with restrictions. Under section 74 of the Bail Act, a further application in the same court generally requires new evidence, new circumstances, or a material change. We can also apply in a higher court — for example, a Supreme Court "release application" after a Local Court refusal. Speed matters; the longer you are in custody, the harder bail becomes.
A breach is treated seriously. Police can arrest you without warrant, the court will be informed, and bail can be revoked entirely — meaning custody until your matter is finalised. Even a small administrative breach (missing a reporting day, being late for curfew) can undo months of careful work. Call us immediately if you think you may have breached.
Not in every matter. Where the court is concerned about flight risk or seriousness of the charge, it may require a surety — a person who guarantees in writing that you will appear, backed by money or property. The amount is set by the court on the day and varies widely. We help you identify a suitable surety in advance so the application is not delayed.
04 · Outcomes & Penalties

Possible outcomes.

The sentencing options available to a NSW court — and what they mean for your record and your future.

A Section 10 (now technically a section 10(1)(a) dismissal or conditional release order without conviction) means the court finds the offence proved but does not record a conviction. There is no criminal record, no fine, no licence disqualification. Whether you are a realistic candidate depends on your character, the seriousness of the offence and the strength of your subjective material — which is exactly what we build in the lead-up to sentencing.
Only if a conviction is recorded. A finding of guilt without a conviction (a Section 10 outcome) does not create a criminal record for most purposes. We will tell you, before you enter a plea, what outcome is realistic and what its impact will be on employment, travel, working-with-children checks and professional licences.
Full-time imprisonment is a sentence of last resort under NSW law. For most offences the court must consider every alternative first — fines, Conditional Release Orders (CROs), Community Correction Orders (CCOs) and Intensive Correction Orders (ICOs, served in the community under strict supervision) — before imposing a custodial sentence. The realistic exposure depends on your charge, your antecedents and the quality of your plea in mitigation.
A Conditional Release Order (CRO) is the lightest — a good-behaviour bond up to 2 years, with or without conviction. A Community Correction Order (CCO) is mid-range, up to 3 years, and can include community service or supervision. An Intensive Correction Order (ICO) is a sentence of imprisonment up to 2 years served in the community under intensive supervision — it is, legally, a jail sentence, just not one served behind bars.
Yes. You have 28 days from the date of sentence to appeal a Local Court decision to the District Court — either against conviction, against severity of sentence, or both. The appeal is a fresh hearing. The window is strict, so if you are unhappy with the outcome, call us within days, not weeks.
It can. A recorded conviction may appear on a National Police Check, can be a disqualifying factor for certain professional registrations (legal, medical, financial, teaching, security), and can affect visas for the US, Canada and other countries. We weigh these collateral consequences when we make submissions to the court — they are not "extra"; they are a core part of the case for a Section 10.
05 · Traffic & Licence Matters

Driving offences & your licence.

Drink driving, licence suspensions, demerit appeals and serious driving charges.

For most suspensions — including P1 and P2 demerit suspensions and "speed over 30 km/h" police suspensions — you have 28 days from the notice to lodge a Local Court appeal. The court can set aside the suspension entirely, or impose a shorter period. Acting quickly is critical: a missed deadline ends the appeal.
Most drink driving offences carry an automatic disqualification that the court can reduce to a minimum — and in some matters a Section 10 (no conviction, no disqualification) is achievable. The right outcome depends on your reading, prior record, the reasons for driving and what you have done since the charge (interlock pre-installation, traffic offender program, character references).
For most mid-range, high-range and repeat drink driving offences, NSW law requires an interlock device to be fitted to any vehicle you drive for a minimum period (commonly 12–48 months) after a reduced disqualification. We can explain whether the program applies to your charge, the costs, and whether an interlock exemption order is realistic in your case.
Yes — but you have to put the evidence in front of the court. A letter from your employer, a description of your duties, public transport options, family responsibilities and any exceptional hardship are all weighed on sentence. We help you assemble that material so the court has a complete picture before deciding on disqualification.
