Common Assault · s.61 Crimes Act

Common assault — the broadest assault charge.

Section 61 of the Crimes Act 1900 creates the offence of common assault — and the legal definition is far broader than most people imagine. The Crown does not need to prove any actual injury, and in many cases does not even need to prove physical contact.

What the Crown must prove

  • An act — applying force, attempting force, or threatening force.
  • Done intentionally or recklessly — accidental contact is not assault.
  • Without consent — touching in ordinary social interaction does not count.
  • Without lawful excuse — self-defence, lawful authority, consent in sport.

What actually counts as assault

The threshold is low — but it is the threshold. The following will all support a common assault charge in NSW:

  • A push, slap or shove in anger.
  • Spitting on another person.
  • A raised fist at close range — even without contact.
  • Verbal threats of imminent harm where the victim reasonably feared it would happen.
  • Throwing an object at a person — even one that misses.

Maximum penalty & sentencing

Common assault carries a maximum penalty of 2 years imprisonment. It is almost always dealt with in the Local Court as a summary matter. In practice the vast majority of first-offence common assault matters resolve well below the maximum — often with a Section 10 dismissal for clients with no prior history and proper mitigation.

Assault Occasioning ABH · s.59

Where contact becomes injury.

Assault Occasioning Actual Bodily Harm sits above common assault on the seriousness scale. The dividing line is the injury — and the Crown only needs to prove that the injury was more than transient or trifling.

What is "actual bodily harm"?

ABH is defined by the common law as any hurt or injury that interferes with the health or comfort of the victim, provided that the hurt is more than merely transient or trifling. The bar is therefore low — but it is real:

  • Bruises, swelling and minor cuts — clearly within the definition.
  • Scratches that leave a mark — usually included.
  • Black eyes, split lips and grazes — clearly included.
  • Recognised psychological injury (such as a post-incident anxiety disorder) can amount to ABH in NSW case law.
  • Knock-out punches that leave only momentary effect — still ABH.

The penalty framework

  • s.59(1) — Assault occasioning ABH: maximum 5 years imprisonment.
  • s.59(2) — Assault occasioning ABH in company: maximum 7 years imprisonment.

Local or District Court?

ABH is a Table 1 offence under the Criminal Procedure Act 1986. That means it is dealt with summarily in the Local Court unless either the prosecution or the defence elects for trial on indictment in the District Court. The election is strategic — Local Court keeps the maximum sentence available to the Magistrate at 2 years; District Court opens the full 5 (or 7) years.

Domestic violence overlay

Where the victim is in a domestic relationship with the accused, ABH is flagged as a domestic violence offence. That flag changes how bail is approached, usually attracts an interim AVO, and shapes the sentencing principles applied.

Affray · Section 93C

When violence becomes a public offence.

Section 93C of the Crimes Act 1900 creates the offence of affray — an offence directed not just at the immediate victim, but at the community at large. Affray is a District Court matter and carries a real custodial range.

Crimes Act · s.93C
Affray
10 Years Max
District Court matter. Custodial range from the outset on contested allegations.
Public Order Offence
Reasonable Firmness Test
Crown Burden
Crown must establish a hypothetical bystander would have feared for safety.

What the Crown must prove

  • Use or threat of unlawful violence by the accused.
  • Towards another — there must be a victim or target.
  • The conduct would cause a person of reasonable firmness present at the scene to fear for their personal safety.
  • Mere words alone are not enough — there must be conduct.

Multiple offenders treated as a single course of conduct

Where two or more people use or threaten violence together, the section operates so that the conduct of the group is assessed as a whole. This is why affray is the characteristic charge for group altercations outside venues, brawls, and organised confrontations.

The "person of reasonable firmness" test

No actual bystander needs to be at the scene. The test is hypothetical — would a person of reasonable firmness, if present, have feared for their personal safety? The fictional bystander is an objective test, and Crown evidence is directed at it.

Why affray is so often the contested charge

Affray is frequently charged alongside common assault or ABH where the actual contact is hard to prove. From the Crown's perspective, the public-order character of the offence means a successful prosecution depends on the scene, not just on the interaction with the victim. From the defence side, this opens the door to attacking the Crown's reconstruction of the scene — CCTV, witness statements, the geography of the venue, and the conduct of the alleged group.

Reckless Wounding · Section 35

The most serious assault charges.

Section 35 of the Crimes Act 1900 creates a tiered set of offences for the most serious assault matters in NSW. The distinctions matter — every tier carries a different maximum and a different sentencing trajectory.

What is a "wound"?

A "wound" is defined narrowly in NSW law as the breaking of both layers of the skin — the epidermis and the dermis. A bruise is not a wound. A graze that draws blood is. Crown medical evidence on this point can determine whether the charge holds at all.

What is "grievous bodily harm"?

GBH is defined by case law as "really serious injury" — broken bones, permanent or long-lasting damage, serious psychological harm, or any injury that is life-threatening in its character. The standard is meaningfully higher than ABH.

Recklessness vs intent

The distinction between section 35 (reckless) and section 33 (intent) is critical:

  • Reckless wounding / GBH — the accused foresaw the possibility of some harm (wound or ABH at minimum) but proceeded anyway.
  • Wounding / GBH with intent — the accused specifically intended to cause the wounding or GBH that resulted.

The Crown must prove the relevant mental element to the relevant standard. On the right facts, an intent charge can be reduced to a recklessness charge — meaning a meaningfully lower sentencing exposure.

Aggravating factors across all assault offences

Sentencing courts treat the following as aggravating across the assault hierarchy. Their presence can lift a matter from a Section 10 candidate into a custodial sentencing range:

  • In company — multiple offenders, acting in concert.
  • Use of a weapon — even an improvised one.
  • Victim a police officer or emergency services worker.
  • Vulnerable victim — elderly, disabled, child, intoxicated.
  • Domestic relationship — DV-flagged matters carry their own framework.
  • Premeditation — planned attack rather than spontaneous reaction.
  • Public place / public order impact — venue or transport setting.

The decision to prosecute belongs to the Crown, not the complainant. Once police have laid a charge, the matter proceeds through the court system — even if the complainant withdraws cooperation. The complainant's position can affect the strength of the Crown case (and your defence strategy), but it is not determinative. For domestic violence-flagged matters, the Crown will routinely subpoena reluctant witnesses.
Self-defence is governed by section 418 of the Crimes Act 1900. You act in self-defence if you believe the conduct is necessary to defend yourself (or another), to protect property, or to prevent unlawful entry to property — and the response is reasonable in the circumstances as you perceived them. The Crown bears the burden of disproving self-defence beyond reasonable doubt once it is raised. It is a complete defence — meaning a not-guilty verdict.
