Overview · Section 61I

What is sexual assault under NSW law?

Section 61I of the Crimes Act 1900 defines sexual assault as where a person has sexual intercourse with another person without the consent of that other person — and knows that the other person does not consent. It is one of the most serious offences in New South Wales.

"Sexual intercourse" is given a broad statutory meaning. It includes penetration of the genitalia of a woman or the anus of any person by a body part, or by any object manipulated by a person. The Crown must prove every element beyond reasonable doubt.

The three elements the Crown must prove

Each element invites a distinct line of defence, and each demands careful evidence-led strategy from the outset:

  • Sexual intercourse occurred — as defined in the Act.
  • It occurred without the complainant's consent — under the affirmative consent model now in force in NSW.
  • The accused knew, was reckless, or had no reasonable grounds to believe that consent was given.

Because allegations are often unwitnessed and rely heavily on credibility, the case you build in the first 48 hours can shape the entire prosecution.

Aggravating Circumstances · Section 61J

When the charge becomes aggravated.

The Crown may elect to charge under s.61J — increasing the maximum penalty to 20 years — where any one or more of the following circumstances of aggravation is alleged:

  • Multiple persons — the offence is committed in the company of another.
  • Victim under 16 — including children under the special-care provisions.
  • Actual bodily harm caused or threatened — including with a weapon.
  • Under authority — the victim was under the offender's care or authority.
  • Cognitive or physical impairment of the victim.
  • Break and enter committed to facilitate the offence.
  • Deprivation of liberty — the victim was deprived of their liberty.
  • Threat of harm to the victim or another person present.
Section 61KC · 61KD

Sexual touching offences.

A separate but related offence in the NSW Crimes Act, covering non-consensual sexual contact that falls short of intercourse.

Under Section 61KC, sexual touching occurs where a person, without consent, intentionally sexually touches another person — or incites another to do so. It includes the deliberate, unlawful touching of another person with any part of the body, or with anything else, where that touching has a sexual connotation or overtone.

The most common alleged conduct involves touching of the anus, breasts, buttocks, or genitalia, or any conduct a reasonable person would regard as sexual in the circumstances. The aggravated form under Section 61KD applies where one or more circumstances of aggravation are present — mirroring the s.61J list above.

Sexual touching is generally heard in the Local Court, where the maximum penalty is 2 years imprisonment or a $5,500 fine. The District Court hears more serious matters, where the maximum rises to 5 years (s.61KC) or 7 years (s.61KD).
