Provisional Licence Suspensions

P1 & P2 drivers — a tighter margin.

Provisional drivers in NSW operate on a materially lower demerit point threshold than full-licence drivers. Hit the threshold and Transport for NSW issues an automatic Notice of Suspension. The threshold is not flexible — and the only avenue left is a Local Court appeal.

The thresholds

  • P1 (red plates) — exceed 4 demerit points within the licence period and TfNSW automatically suspends for three months.
  • P2 (green plates) — exceed 7 demerit points and the same automatic three-month suspension applies.
  • Any speed offence on a P-plate — even a single km/h over the limit attracts a demerit penalty significantly heavier than on a full licence.

Why provisional drivers are at higher risk

Several factors compound the risk for P1 and P2 holders:

  • Lower thresholds mean a single mistake can trigger suspension — there is no "buffer" of points the way a full licence has 13.
  • Heavier penalty schedules for the same offence — a low-range speed offence that earns 1 point on a full licence can earn 4 on a P1.
  • P1 drivers face mobile phone, passenger and speed-specific restrictions that a full-licence driver does not — breach of any of these is a separate offence and a separate suspension trigger.
  • Cumulative effect — points incurred on a learner or P1 licence are carried into the P2 period and beyond.

Your right to appeal

Section 267 of the Road Transport Act 2013 provides a statutory right to appeal a Transport for NSW suspension to the Local Court. The court can set aside the suspension entirely, vary the period, or dismiss the appeal. The window for lodging is 28 days from the date of the Notice of Suspension.

Speed-Related Suspensions

Speed over 30 km/h — automatic suspension.

Two thresholds. Two different responses. Both attract an automatic Transport for NSW suspension — and both can be appealed.

NSW imposes automatic suspensions on full-licence drivers caught well above the posted limit. There are two tiers:

  • Over 30 km/h but not more than 45 km/h — a Notice of Suspension is issued by Transport for NSW carrying a 3-month suspension.
  • Over 45 km/h — Police can issue an on-the-spot suspension at the roadside, surrendering your licence immediately. Default suspension period is 6 months.

How the suspension is delivered

For offences detected by camera (mobile speed camera, fixed point camera, average-speed camera), the Notice of Suspension is issued by Transport for NSW and posted to the licence-holder. The suspension takes effect on the date specified in the notice — usually 21 to 28 days after issue.

For offences detected by police at the roadside above 45 km/h, the licence is physically surrendered on the spot and the suspension takes effect immediately.

The 28-day appeal window

For both pathways, you have 28 days from the date of the suspension notice (or the date the police took the licence) to lodge an appeal in the Local Court under section 267. The application is filed at the registry of the Local Court closest to where the offence occurred.

Stay of suspension pending appeal

For drivers facing serious livelihood impact, the court can grant a stay of the suspension — allowing you to keep driving while the appeal is determined. The stay is not automatic and requires its own application with supporting evidence on the day of filing.

The Appeal Process

From notice to court decision.

The Local Court appeal is a four-step process. Each step matters — particularly the first, where the 28-day clock is running from the moment you read the suspension notice.

Step 01

Contact us within 28 days

Free first consultation. We confirm the deadline, the court of filing, and the strength of an appeal on your facts.

Step 02

Build the brief

Statutory declaration, character references, employment letter, driving record, financial impact — every document that establishes exceptional hardship.

Step 03

File appeal & seek stay

Application lodged at the Local Court — combined where appropriate with a stay application allowing you to drive pending the hearing.

Step 04

Court hearing & outcome

An Accredited Specialist appears for you. The Magistrate can set aside the suspension entirely, reduce it, or confirm the original period.

What the Magistrate considers

On a section 267 appeal the court weighs exceptional hardship, the seriousness of the offence, your driving record, your character, and the likely impact of the suspension on you and your dependants. We frame every appeal around these four pillars:

  • Need for licence — work, family obligations, medical or carer requirements with no realistic alternative.
  • Driving record — placed in context: years of safe driving, isolated incident, distance travelled.
  • Character — references from employers, community leaders, treating professionals where relevant.
  • Mitigation — defensive-driving courses completed, financial impact evidence, vehicle alternatives explored.
Our Defence Approach

How we win licence appeals.

Licence appeals are decided on the strength of the evidentiary brief. The Magistrate decides on paper as much as on submissions — which is why our preparation is exhaustive and our appearances are tightly focused.
