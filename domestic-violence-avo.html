Overview

When does a charge become domestic?

Under NSW law, an offence is classified as a domestic violence offence when it is committed against a person with whom the accused has a domestic relationship. That relationship is defined broadly under the Crimes (Domestic and Personal Violence) Act 2007 and covers far more than just current intimate partners.

The label is significant. A domestic violence flag on a charge changes how police investigate, how bail is approached, what conditions are imposed, and how the matter is sentenced. It is also almost always accompanied by an interim AVO made on the spot.

Who counts as in a "domestic relationship"

  • Current partner — married, de facto or intimate partner.
  • Former partner — including ex-spouses and ex-partners.
  • Family member — parents, children, siblings, in-laws.
  • Household member — including housemates and long-term residents.
  • Carer or person being cared for — paid or unpaid.
  • Relative under Aboriginal & Torres Strait Islander kinship rules.

Forms domestic violence can take

The law recognises that domestic violence is broader than physical assault. It includes verbal, psychological, sexual, emotional and financial abuse — and, since 2024 reforms, coercive control in qualifying intimate-partner relationships.

Apprehended Violence Orders

What an AVO does — and what it does not.

An AVO is a civil protective order. It is not a criminal conviction in itself, but breaching one is — and the consequences for work, firearms and family life are real from the day it is made.

There are two types under the Crimes (Domestic and Personal Violence) Act 2007:

  • ADVO (Apprehended Domestic Violence Order) — where the parties are in a domestic relationship. Applied for by Police in almost every charged matter.
  • APVO (Apprehended Personal Violence Order) — where the parties are not in a domestic relationship (neighbours, colleagues, acquaintances).

Interim vs Final

An interim order is made on the spot — usually when a DV charge is laid — and runs until the matter is determined. A final order is made after a hearing (or by consent) and ordinarily runs for 12 to 24 months, longer in serious matters.

The three standard conditions

Every AVO contains three mandatory conditions prohibiting the defendant from:

  • Assaulting, threatening, stalking, harassing or intimidating the protected person.
  • Intentionally or recklessly destroying or damaging property belonging to or in the possession of the protected person.
  • Behaving in a manner that interferes with the protected person.

Police or the protected person can ask the court for additional conditions — no contact, no approach within a specified distance of the home or workplace, no contact via third parties or social media, no possession of firearms, and (where relevant) exclusion from a shared home.

If you share children

You must tell the Magistrate if you have children with the protected person and whether there are any family law parenting orders or agreements in place. AVO conditions can override existing parenting arrangements unless the order is specifically tailored to allow contact for handover.

Collateral consequences

Even without a criminal conviction, an AVO triggers an automatic 10-year firearms prohibition, can affect working with children checks and certain professional registrations, and is disclosable for some employment background checks.

Breach of AVO · Section 14

Contravention is a criminal offence.

The AVO itself is civil. Breaching it is not. Under section 14 of the Crimes (DPV) Act 2007, contravention is a stand-alone criminal charge — often treated more seriously than the underlying matter that brought the order into being.

Crimes (DPV) · s.14
Contravention of AVO
2 Years Max
+ 50 penalty units (~$5,500). Custodial penalty mandatory where the breach involves an act of violence.
Repeat breaches
Aggravated Breach
Likely Custodial
Multiple breaches and breaches by violence almost always attract gaol.

What counts as a breach

  • Direct contact with the protected person — call, text, message, email.
  • Indirect contact through a friend or relative ("just tell them I'm sorry").
  • Attending the home or workplace in breach of an exclusion zone.
  • Social media contact — including liking or commenting on a post.
  • Possession of a firearm while subject to a prohibition condition.
  • Any conduct that the standard three conditions prohibit.

The "reasonable excuse" defence

Section 14(4) of the Act provides a partial defence where the contravention had a reasonable excuse. The most common is unintended attendance — for example, unknowingly turning up at the same venue. The bar is high and the evidence must support the explanation. We have run successful s.14(4) defences where the conduct was unintentional, the protected person initiated contact, or the breach was technical.

Why breaches are treated seriously

Courts treat breach of an AVO as an offence against the administration of justice as much as an offence against the protected person. Where the breach involves any act of violence, the legislation imposes a statutory presumption of a custodial penalty — meaning the Magistrate must impose gaol unless the contrary is shown.
