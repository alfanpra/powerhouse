Local Court Bail

The first — and most important — bail application.

After arrest, you will be brought before a Magistrate as soon as practicable — usually the next day, sometimes the same day if you are arrested in the morning. This is your first opportunity to apply for bail in court, and under the Bail Act 2013 you are entitled to one bail application as of right in the Local Court.

Police bail vs Court bail

Bail can be granted by police at the station or by a court. Police bail is decided by the custody manager and can be granted on conditions; refusing it forces the matter to court. Court bail is decided by the Magistrate on the first appearance and is the more substantive application — it is where the real case for release is made.

The two statutory tests

Every bail application in NSW is decided against two related tests under the Bail Act 2013:

  • The "show cause" test (s.16A & 16B) — for "show cause" offences, the accused must first show cause why their detention is not justified before the court will even consider unacceptable risk.
  • The "unacceptable risk" test (s.17–19) — applies to every bail application. The court asks whether there is an unacceptable risk of failing to appear, committing a serious offence, endangering safety, or interfering with witnesses.

What the Magistrate considers

Section 18 sets out the factors the court must take into account — including the nature and seriousness of the charge, the strength of the Crown case, your criminal record, your ties to the community, any history of bail breaches, and conditions that might mitigate risk.

One shot — make it count

Section 74 of the Act restricts further applications in the Local Court: once refused, you cannot reapply unless there are new facts or new circumstances that were not raised in the first application. A poorly prepared first application can lock you out of relief until District or Supreme Court review.

District Court Bail

A fresh hearing — with a real chance.

For indictable matters committed for trial or sentence, the District Court takes over. For matters refused bail in the Local Court, the District Court is the next forum — and a chance to put a stronger application before a Judge.

The District Court hears bail applications in two situations:

  • Fresh bail applications on indictable matters that have been committed for trial or sentence from the Local Court.
  • De novo bail review following refusal in the Local Court — the application is heard afresh, not as an appeal in the strict sense.

Different forum, different submissions

The District Court application requires significantly more preparation than the Local Court appearance. We typically file:

  • A written bail submission addressing the show cause and unacceptable risk tests on the specific facts of the matter.
  • Statutory declarations from a proposed surety, residence, employer, and any treating health professionals.
  • A proposed conditions schedule tailored to mitigate every risk the Crown is likely to raise.
  • Where useful, an expert risk assessment or psychological report.

Why a written brief works

A written application allows the Judge to read the case for release in its strongest form before submissions begin — which is rarely possible in the Local Court's faster-moving list. We have secured bail in the District Court on matters that were refused in the Local Court because the written brief reframed the risk profile.

Variation of existing bail

The District Court also entertains variation applications — to change conditions, address surety substitution, or accommodate changes in employment or residence. These applications can be lodged on shorter notice and are heard in chambers in some matters.

The "new facts or new circumstances" test

Under section 74 of the Bail Act, a repeated bail application is only permitted where there has been a material change in circumstances, new information that was not before the earlier court, or where the application was made without legal representation. The submissions must be specific — generic improvements are not enough.

What "new circumstances" typically looks like

  • An approved place of residence not available at the time of the earlier application.
  • A written employment offer from a credible employer.
  • A substantial surety not previously offered, with bank or property security.
  • Health information — treatment plan, psychological report, medical letter — establishing rehabilitative basis.
  • Delay — significant time elapsed between charge and trial making continued detention oppressive.
  • A weakening of the Crown brief — new evidence undermining the case against the accused.

Defending a detention application

Where the Crown brings a detention application under section 74, the burden is on the Crown to show why your bail should be revoked. We approach these matters with the same rigour as a defended hearing — testing the Crown's risk submissions, deploying independent character and surety evidence, and offering revised conditions where useful.

Bail — FAQ.

The questions we hear most often in the first conversation about bail — usually from a partner or family member of the person in custody.

Yes — but with restrictions. Under section 74 of the Bail Act, a second application in the same court (or a higher court) requires new facts or new circumstances that were not before the earlier court. Without that change, the application will be summarily refused. That is why the first application must be done properly.
A show cause offence is one listed in section 16B of the Bail Act where the accused must first show cause why their continued detention is not justified before the court will consider the unacceptable risk test. The list includes murder, serious sexual offences, serious drug offences (commercial quantity), firearms offences, and offending while on bail or parole.
A surety is a person who guarantees in writing that the accused will appear in court — and who deposits or pledges money or property as security. If the accused fails to appear, the surety forfeits the deposit. The amount depends on the seriousness of the charge — from a few thousand dollars in minor matters up to ,000 or more in serious indictable cases.
A Local Court application typically resolves the same day — the matter is listed at the first appearance, the application is run, and the decision is delivered before the court rises. District and Supreme Court applications are listed within days, with written submissions filed in advance. Urgent Supreme Court applications can be heard within hours.
A breach is a serious matter. Police can arrest you without warrant. The court will be informed and your bail may be revoked entirely, forfeiting any surety and returning you to custody. Even a technical breach (missing a reporting requirement, late return from a permitted absence) can be enough to revoke bail — particularly on serious indictable matters.
