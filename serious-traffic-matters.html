Negligent Driving · s.117 RTA

The three tiers of negligent driving.

Section 117 of the Road Transport Act 2013 creates a single offence — negligent driving — with three sentencing tiers that scale dramatically with the consequences of the driving. The standard is straightforward: did the manner of driving fall below what a reasonable and prudent driver would have done in the same circumstances?

  • Negligent driving (simpliciter) — no injury caused. A fine-only offence, no automatic disqualification, but demerit points and a criminal record on conviction.
  • Negligent driving occasioning grievous bodily harm (GBH) — serious injury caused. Real prison exposure and automatic disqualification.
  • Negligent driving occasioning death — the most serious tier. Custodial sentencing range starts at 18 months and disqualification at 3 years automatic.

Negligence does not require intent. A momentary lapse — checking a phone, looking across at a passenger, misjudging a gap — is enough. What separates the tiers is outcome, not culpability.

What "Negligent" Means

The legal standard — reasonable and prudent.

The prosecution does not have to prove that you intended to drive badly, only that the driving fell short of the standard expected of a reasonable and prudent driver in the same circumstances. Common factual patterns include:

  • Distraction — phone use, in-car screens, eating, smoking, passenger interaction.
  • Failure to keep a proper lookout — most rear-end collisions and intersection failures fall here.
  • Excessive speed for the conditions — even at or below the posted limit (wet, fog, poor visibility).
  • Fatigue — particularly on long highway drives or shift-work returns.
  • Misjudging a turn or merge — gap selection, blind-spot failures, opposing traffic.

Where the alleged negligence is marginal — i.e. arguably within the normal range of driver error — there is genuine room to defend the charge, not merely plead in mitigation.

Dangerous Driving · s.52A Crimes Act

Dangerous driving.

Dangerous driving is prosecuted under the Crimes Act 1900, not the Road Transport Act — and the penalty exposure reflects that. These are indictable charges, often committed to the District Court, with sentencing ranges that begin in years of imprisonment, not months.

The four base offences

  • Dangerous driving occasioning death (s.52A(1)) — max 10 years.
  • Dangerous driving occasioning grievous bodily harm (s.52A(3)) — max 7 years.
  • Aggravated dangerous driving occasioning death (s.52A(2)) — max 14 years.
  • Aggravated dangerous driving occasioning GBH (s.52A(4)) — max 11 years.

What makes it "dangerous"

The Crown must prove the vehicle was being driven in a manner dangerous to another person — an objective standard. The driving must have created a real and serious risk to others, judged against all the circumstances: speed, road conditions, traffic density, visibility, the vehicle's condition.

The aggravating factors

A charge is uplifted to the aggravated form where any of the following are made out at the time of the driving:

  • Prescribed concentration of alcohol at or above 0.15 (high range).
  • Driving very substantially in excess of the speed limit — the "very substantially" threshold is fact-dependent but commonly applied above 45 km/h over.
  • Driving to escape pursuit by police.
  • Driving while very substantially impaired by drugs or a combination of alcohol and drugs.

Police pursuits — Skye's Law (s.51B)

A related offence with its own custodial range: driving recklessly, furiously or at a dangerous speed while being pursued by police. First offence carries up to 3 years; second or subsequent up to 5 years and a 5-year automatic disqualification. These charges are almost always run alongside dangerous-driving counts.

What the court considers on sentencing

On a plea or finding of guilt, the court weighs the manner of driving (the higher the recklessness, the higher the sentence), the consequences (catastrophic injury or multiple victims uplift the case), the driver's history, any voluntary impairment, and genuine remorse and rehabilitation demonstrated by the time of sentence. A dangerous-driving conviction almost invariably carries a custodial element — the work is in establishing the strongest available case for a non-custodial alternative such as an Intensive Correction Order (ICO).

Drive Whilst Suspended · s.54 RTA

Drive whilst suspended.

Being caught driving with a suspended licence is treated as a serious traffic offence in NSW — even where the underlying suspension was administrative (demerit points, unpaid fines, medical) rather than the result of a court order.

The "honest and reasonable mistake" defence

A genuine, demonstrable defence under section 54 is that you did not know, and had no reason to know, the licence was suspended — for example, because a notice was never received, was sent to an old address, or was concealed by a household member. The defence is not easy, but where the facts support it, the charge fails entirely. Document everything — proof of address changes, mail handling, RMS contact history.

Why a Section 10 matters here

A conviction for drive-while-suspended creates a compounding problem — the court-imposed disqualification adds to the existing suspension, your insurance cover usually lapses, and a second offence within five years carries a presumption against bail-style leniency. Securing a Section 10 on a first offence keeps the traffic record clean and avoids the cascade.

Demerit suspension and the appeal pathway

Where the suspension itself flowed from a demerit-point accumulation, there is often a parallel Licence Appeal route worth running before the drive-while-suspended matter reaches sentence. See our Licence Appeals page — a successful appeal can reduce or eliminate the underlying suspension and recontextualise the new charge.

Drive Whilst Disqualified · s.54 RTA

Drive whilst disqualified.

Driving during a court-imposed disqualification period is the most serious form of the section 54 family. The court treats it as a defiance of an existing court order — and on a second offence, custodial sentences are the norm, not the exception.

How disqualified differs from suspended

Suspension is usually administrative — Transport for NSW removes licence privileges for points, fines or medical reasons. Disqualification is judicial — a Magistrate or Judge orders it as part of a sentence. The court takes the breach of a judicial order more seriously than an administrative one, and the sentencing range reflects that.

The cumulative-disqualification trap

A new disqualification imposed for drive-while-disqualified runs from the end of the existing one — not concurrently. A second drive-while-disqualified charge during the same disqualification period can therefore push the total off-road period out by years. We routinely see clients accumulate 10+ years of stacked disqualifications without ever returning to the road.

The Habitual Traffic Offender declaration

Three relevant traffic convictions within a five-year period exposes a driver to a Habitual Traffic Offender (HTO) declaration, which adds a further 5-year disqualification on top of any other penalty. HTO declarations can be quashed by the court on application — a critical step that we run alongside sentencing where the criteria are met.

Custodial exposure on a second offence

On a second drive-while-disqualified within five years, the realistic starting point is a full-time custodial sentence. The mitigation work — character, rehabilitation, the underlying necessity of the driving, mental health background, steps already taken — has to be done thoroughly and early, ideally before the matter is listed for sentence.

Our Defence Approach

How we defend serious traffic matters.

These charges are won by attacking the brief early and building a sentencing case in parallel. We do both from day one — not in the last fortnight before court.

01
Section 10 strategyFor negligent driving and first-offence drive-while-suspended matters, a Section 10 dismissal preserves your licence, your record and your insurance position. We pursue it as the primary outcome wherever the facts will support it.
02
Challenging the negligence standardThe "reasonable and prudent driver" test is fact-specific. Witness reliability, road geometry, weather, vehicle condition and reaction-time evidence can all reposition a marginal negligence case.
03
Honest and reasonable mistakeFor drive-while-suspended charges, demonstrable lack of knowledge of the suspension is a complete defence — provided the underlying facts and documentation support it.
04
Charge negotiationA dangerous-driving charge can sometimes be negotiated down to negligent driving where the manner of driving is open to characterisation either way. The difference is years of custodial exposure.
05
ICO and CCO submissionsWhere conviction is unavoidable on a dangerous-driving matter, the work shifts to Intensive Correction Orders or Community Correction Orders — community-served alternatives to full-time custody.
06
HTO quashing applicationsWhere a Habitual Traffic Offender declaration is in play, we apply to have it quashed on the basis of disproportionality — preventing a further 5-year disqualification from stacking onto the sentence.
